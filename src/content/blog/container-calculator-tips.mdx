Every Nigerian importer wants to get the most value out of every shipment. Whether you are shipping a single carton or a full container, understanding how shipping lines calculate charges — and how to maximise the space you pay for — can lead to significant savings.

## Understanding Container Types and Capacities

Before calculating what fits, you need to know the standard container sizes used in sea freight.

**20-foot container (20ft DC):** Internal dimensions approximately 5.9m x 2.35m x 2.39m. Usable capacity around 28-30 cubic metres (CBM). Maximum payload roughly 25,000kg.

**40-foot container (40ft DC):** Internal dimensions approximately 12.03m x 2.35m x 2.39m. Usable capacity around 58-60 CBM. Maximum payload roughly 28,000kg.

**40-foot high cube (40ft HC):** Same length and width as a standard 40ft, but taller at 2.69m internal height. Usable capacity around 65-68 CBM. Ideal for bulky, lightweight cargo.

## How Volumetric Weight Affects Your Costs

For air freight and less-than-container-load (LCL) sea shipments, shipping lines use the **volumetric weight** formula to determine chargeable weight when cargo is light but bulky.

The formula is straightforward: **Length × Width × Height (in cm) ÷ 5000 = Volumetric Weight in kg.**

If your shipment weighs 50kg but occupies 100kg of volumetric space, you pay for 100kg. This is why compact packing directly reduces your shipping cost.

## Practical Tips for Maximising Container Space

**Use uniform carton sizes.** When cartons are all the same size, they stack neatly without wasting gap space. Mixed sizes create voids that either waste space or require additional dunnage to fill.

**Consider disassembly.** Products that can be partially disassembled — like furniture, shelving, or equipment — often pack much more efficiently when broken down into flat components.

**Plan your pallet configuration.** Standard pallets measure 1.2m x 1.0m or 1.2m x 0.8m. Planning your carton dimensions to fit these pallet sizes evenly reduces wasted space and improves handling.

**Use the full height.** Many shippers underutilise the vertical space inside a container. Stacking cartons to the full internal height, within safe load limits, can increase utilisation by 20% or more.

## Calculating Your Chargeable Weight

Before shipping, always run both calculations:

1. **Actual weight** — what the cargo weighs on a scale
2. **Volumetric weight** — the space the cargo occupies

The higher of the two becomes your **chargeable weight**, which is what the carrier uses to calculate the freight charge.

> Example: A carton measuring 80cm × 60cm × 50cm and weighing 35kg. Volumetric weight = (80 × 60 × 50) ÷ 5000 = 48kg. Chargeable weight = 48kg (the higher figure).
