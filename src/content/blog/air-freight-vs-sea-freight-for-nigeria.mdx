Air freight works best for urgent, valuable, or lightweight products. It helps businesses restock faster and can protect margins when the product turns quickly after arrival. If you're importing gadgets, fashion items, or highly requested seasonal goods, the extra cost of air freight is often offset by the rapid speed to market.

## The Case for Air Freight

Choosing air freight is essentially choosing **speed and reliability**. 

- **Faster transit times:** Typically 10-15 working days from China to Lagos.
- **Lower inventory carrying costs:** Because goods arrive quickly, you don't need to tie up capital for months.
- **Enhanced security:** Airports have strict security controls, reducing the risk of theft or damage.

> "I switched to air freight for my boutique restocks. Even though the shipping cost per kg was higher, my capital turnover doubled because I was selling out twice as fast."

## When to Choose Sea Freight

Sea freight is usually better for bulky, heavy, or high-volume cargo where the lower shipping cost matters significantly more than speed. It suits buyers who can plan ahead, maintain steady inventory buffers, and wait longer for arrival.

- **Cost-effective for heavy goods:** Furniture, machinery, and bulk electronics are almost always shipped by sea.
- **Higher capacity:** You can ship entire containers (FCL) or share space (LCL) depending on your business size.
- **Eco-friendly:** Sea freight has a significantly lower carbon footprint compared to air shipping.

## Making the Final Decision

The right choice depends on your product weight, package size, urgency, and selling timeline. A good shipping plan should protect both delivery speed and landed cost. 

### A Quick Checklist

Before you ship, ask yourself these crucial questions:
- Is my customer waiting for this immediately?
- Will the shipping cost eat up my entire profit margin?
- Do I have enough capital to wait 60+ days for a sea shipment?

Ultimately, many successful importers use a **hybrid approach**—flying in a small batch of goods immediately to test the market, while simultaneously sending the bulk of the order via sea freight.
