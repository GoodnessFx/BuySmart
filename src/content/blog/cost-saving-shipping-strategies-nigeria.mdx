Shipping costs are often the largest variable expense for Nigerian importers. While some costs are fixed — fuel surcharges, port fees, duties — there are several strategies that can meaningfully reduce what you pay per unit shipped.

## Strategy 1: Consolidate Multiple Orders

Instead of shipping one supplier's goods at a time, combine purchases from multiple suppliers into a single shipment. Consolidation reduces the number of individual shipments, lowers per-unit freight costs, and simplifies customs clearance.

**How it works:** Your goods from different suppliers are delivered to a consolidator's warehouse in the origin country. The consolidator combines everything into one shipment, handles export documentation, and books a single container or LCL (less than container load) space.

## Strategy 2: Choose Sea Freight for Heavy or Bulk Cargo

Air freight is fast but expensive — typically 4 to 6 times more costly than sea freight per kilogram. For goods that are not urgently needed, sea freight can reduce your shipping cost by 60% or more.

**When sea freight makes sense:**
- Large or heavy items (furniture, machinery, bulk inventory)
- Products with stable demand where 8-12 week lead time is acceptable
- Lower-margin goods where every naira of shipping cost matters
- Mixed wholesale cargo being imported for resale

## Strategy 3: Optimise Packing to Reduce Volumetric Weight

As discussed in our container calculator guide, volumetric weight can significantly increase shipping costs for bulky but lightweight cargo. Reducing packaging volume — using vacuum sealing for soft goods, disassembling furniture, or choosing more compact carton sizes — directly reduces chargeable weight.

**Quick wins:**
- Request suppliers to use minimal, sturdy packaging
- Specify carton size limits that fit standard pallet dimensions
- Avoid excessive void fill and oversized outer cartons

## Strategy 4: Plan Shipments Around Schedules

Shipping lines and airlines publish sailing and departure schedules months in advance. Booking early secures better rates and guaranteed space. Last-minute bookings during peak seasons (August-October before Chinese Golden Week, or November-December for year-end inventory) can cost 20-30% more.

## Strategy 5: Compare Air and Sea Costs Per Unit

The cheapest option depends on your specific product and sales cycle. A useful exercise is calculating the **total landed cost per unit** for both air and sea, including all freight, duties, clearance, and delivery charges.

Sometimes air freight at a higher per-kg rate results in lower total cost because it avoids warehousing, reduces inventory carrying costs, and accelerates sales. The key is running the numbers for your specific product rather than assuming sea is always cheaper.

By leveraging volume, they secure better per-kg rates from carriers and pass those savings to clients. Working with a procurement partner often costs less than managing shipping independently when you factor in the time, risk, and coordination overhead.
