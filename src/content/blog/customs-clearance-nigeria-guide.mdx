Customs clearance is often the stage that causes the most anxiety for Nigerian importers. The good news is that the process is straightforward when you understand the requirements and have the right documentation in place before your shipment arrives.

## How Nigerian Customs Clearance Works

When your goods arrive at a Nigerian port — typically Apapa or Tin Can Island in Lagos — they enter a customs-controlled area. A licensed clearing agent processes the clearance by submitting documentation, having the cargo inspected if required, and paying the applicable duties and taxes.

**The key players involved:**
- **Nigeria Customs Service (NCS)** — the government authority that assesses and collects duties
- **Licensed clearing agent** — a registered professional who handles the clearance process on your behalf
- **Nigeria Ports Authority (NPA)** — manages port infrastructure and cargo handling
- **Standard Organisation of Nigeria (SON)** — may inspect certain product categories for quality compliance

## Documents Required for Clearance

Having the correct documents ready before the vessel arrives can cut clearance time significantly.

- **Bill of Lading or Airway Bill** — the contract of carriage issued by the shipping line or airline
- **Commercial Invoice** — issued by your supplier showing the value, quantity, and description of goods
- **Packing List** — details what is inside each package or container
- **Form M** — the mandatory import declaration form processed through the Nigeria Single Window portal before shipment
- **Certificate of Origin** — may be required to claim preferential duty rates under trade agreements
- **SONCAP Certificate** — required for regulated product categories to show compliance with Nigerian standards

## Understanding Import Duties and Taxes

Duties are calculated based on the CIF value (Cost, Insurance, and Freight) of your goods. The applicable rate depends on the product category as classified under the HS Code system.

**Common duty elements include:**
- **Import duty** — varies by product category, typically 5% to 35%
- **VAT** — 7.5% on the CIF value plus import duty
- **Levy** — additional charges may apply for specific goods like vehicles, textiles, or alcohol
- **SONCAP fee** — where applicable for regulated products

## Tips for Smooth Clearance

**Work with an experienced agent.** A good clearing agent knows the current procedures, inspection requirements, and documentation standards. They can flag potential issues before they become delays.

**Declare accurately.** Under-declaring the value of goods to reduce duty can lead to penalties, seizure, or blacklisting. Always declare the true transaction value.

**Plan for inspections.** Some shipments are selected for physical examination. This is routine and does not necessarily mean there is a problem, but it can add a few days to the clearance timeline.

**Pre-arrange Form M.** Submit and obtain your Form M approval before the goods are shipped. Trying to process it after the vessel arrives often leads to delays and demurrage charges.
